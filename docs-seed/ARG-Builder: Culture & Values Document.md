# ARG-Builder: Culture & Values Document

## Executive Summary

This document defines ARG-Builder's cultural foundation — the values, operating principles, decision-making frameworks, and rituals that shape how the team works together. Culture is not a poster on the wall; it is the sum of behaviors that are rewarded, tolerated, and punished. At ARG-Builder, culture is a competitive advantage: it attracts exceptional talent, enables fast decision-making, and creates an environment where people do the best work of their careers.

## Core Values

### 1. Craft Over Speed

We build things that last. Every feature, every document, every customer interaction reflects the same standard of quality we expect from our own product. We would rather ship one excellent thing than three mediocre things. This does not mean we move slowly — it means we refuse to ship work we are not proud of.

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Take an extra hour to polish before shipping | Ship "good enough" and plan to fix later |
| Write documentation as you build | Leave documentation for "someday" |
| Review your own work before asking others to review | Throw work over the wall |
| Ask "would I be proud to show this to a customer?" | Rationalize shortcuts with "we'll iterate" |

### 2. Radical Transparency

Information flows freely. We default to sharing context, not hoarding it. Every team member has access to revenue numbers, strategic decisions, customer feedback, and company challenges. We trust people with information because we trust their judgment.

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Share context behind decisions, not just decisions | Make announcements without explanation |
| Post updates in public channels | DM information that the team needs |
| Admit mistakes openly and quickly | Hide problems until they become crises |
| Share customer feedback (positive and negative) verbatim | Filter or sanitize feedback |

### 3. Customer Obsession

Every decision starts with the customer. We do not build features because they are interesting — we build them because they solve real problems for real people. We measure success not by what we ship, but by the outcomes our customers achieve.

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Talk to customers every week (everyone, not just CS) | Assume you know what customers want |
| Measure feature success by customer outcomes | Measure success by shipping velocity |
| Prioritize customer pain over internal convenience | Build what is easiest for engineering |
| Respond to customer issues within hours, not days | Let support tickets age |

### 4. Own the Outcome

We hire adults who take full ownership of their work. Ownership means you do not wait to be told what to do — you identify what needs to happen and make it happen. It means you care about the result, not just your contribution to it. If something is broken and you see it, it is your responsibility to fix it or flag it.

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| See a problem? Fix it or raise it immediately | Say "that's not my job" |
| Follow through until the outcome is achieved | Hand off and forget |
| Take responsibility when things go wrong | Blame circumstances or other teams |
| Propose solutions, not just problems | Wait for someone else to decide |

### 5. Learn Relentlessly

The market moves fast. Our competitors are smart. The only sustainable advantage is learning faster than everyone else. We celebrate curiosity, reward experimentation, and treat failures as data — not disasters.

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Run experiments and share learnings | Avoid risk to protect your track record |
| Ask "what did we learn?" after every project | Only celebrate successes |
| Invest time in skill development weekly | Stay in your comfort zone |
| Share knowledge freely with the team | Hoard expertise |

## Operating Principles

These principles guide daily decision-making when values alone do not provide clear direction.

### Principle 1: Default to Action

When in doubt, act. A good decision made quickly is better than a perfect decision made slowly. We can always course-correct, but we cannot recover lost time. The cost of inaction almost always exceeds the cost of a wrong action that is quickly corrected.

**Decision Speed Framework:**

| Decision Type | Time Allowed | Reversibility |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| Type 1 (irreversible, high-stakes) | Days to weeks | Low — get it right |
| Type 2 (reversible, moderate-stakes) | Hours to days | High — decide and iterate |
| Type 3 (easily reversible, low-stakes) | Minutes to hours | Very high — just do it |

### Principle 2: Small Teams, Big Impact

We stay small intentionally. Small teams move faster, communicate better, and produce higher-quality work. We resist the temptation to throw people at problems. Instead, we give exceptional people exceptional tools and get out of their way.

### Principle 3: Write It Down

If it is not written down, it does not exist. We document decisions, processes, and learnings because writing forces clarity and creates institutional memory. Our internal operations should reflect the same standard we sell to customers.

### Principle 4: Disagree and Commit

Healthy disagreement produces better decisions. We encourage debate, diverse perspectives, and constructive challenge. But once a decision is made, everyone commits fully — regardless of whether they agreed with the outcome. Passive resistance is not tolerated.

### Principle 5: Progress Over Perfection

We ship iteratively. Version 1 is never perfect, and that is acceptable. What is not acceptable is version 1 that is sloppy, thoughtless, or embarrassing. The standard is: "Is this the best we can do given the constraints?" If yes, ship it. If no, keep working.

## Decision-Making Framework

### RAPID Model

For significant decisions, we use the RAPID framework to ensure clarity of roles:

| Role | Responsibility | Who |
|------|---------------|-----|
| **R**ecommend | Proposes the decision with analysis | Subject matter expert |
| **A**gree | Must agree before decision proceeds (veto power) | Affected stakeholders |
| **P**erform | Executes the decision once made | Implementation team |
| **I**nput | Provides information and perspective | Anyone with relevant knowledge |
| **D**ecide | Makes the final call | Single decision-maker (DRI) |

### Directly Responsible Individual (DRI)

Every project, initiative, and decision has exactly one DRI. The DRI is not necessarily the most senior person — they are the person best positioned to drive the outcome. The DRI has authority to make decisions within their scope without seeking approval.

## Hiring for Culture

### Culture Interview Questions

| Value | Question | What to Listen For |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| Craft Over Speed | "Tell me about something you built that you're genuinely proud of. What made it special?" | Attention to detail, pride in quality, specific examples |
| Radical Transparency | "Describe a time you shared bad news with your team. How did you handle it?" | Honesty, directness, willingness to be vulnerable |
| Customer Obsession | "Tell me about a time you went beyond your job description to help a customer." | Empathy, initiative, outcome-focus |
| Own the Outcome | "Describe a project that failed. What was your role and what did you do?" | Accountability, learning, no blame |
| Learn Relentlessly | "What's the most recent skill you taught yourself? How did you approach it?" | Curiosity, self-direction, growth mindset |

### Culture Add vs. Culture Fit

We hire for **culture add**, not culture fit. We want people who share our values but bring diverse perspectives, backgrounds, and thinking styles. Homogeneity is the enemy of innovation.

## Performance & Accountability

### Performance Dimensions

| Dimension | Weight | Measurement |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Results | 40% | Did you achieve your objectives? |
| Craft | 25% | Was the quality of your work exceptional? |
| Values | 20% | Did you exemplify our values consistently? |
| Growth | 15% | Did you learn, improve, and help others grow? |

### Feedback Culture

Feedback is continuous, not annual. We practice:

| Type | Frequency | Format |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Real-time feedback | Daily | Slack, in-person, after meetings |
| Structured 1:1s | Weekly | Manager + direct report |
| Peer feedback | Monthly | Written, specific, actionable |
| 360 review | Bi-annually | Comprehensive multi-source |
| Performance review | Annually | Formal, tied to compensation |

## Remote Work Philosophy

ARG-Builder operates as a remote-first company with intentional in-person connection. Remote work is not a perk — it is a deliberate choice that enables us to hire the best people regardless of geography and gives everyone the autonomy to do deep work.

| Principle | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Async-first communication | Write > call. Document > discuss. |
| Overlap hours | 4 hours of required overlap (10 AM–2 PM ET) |
| In-person connection | Quarterly offsites, optional co-working |
| Home office support | $2,000 setup budget + $100/month stipend |
| No meeting days | Wednesday = no meetings (deep work day) |
| Trust over surveillance | No tracking software, no mandatory camera-on |
